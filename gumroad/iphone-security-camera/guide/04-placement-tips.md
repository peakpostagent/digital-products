# Chapter 4: Placement Tips

Where you place your camera is just as important as which app you use. Good placement means better coverage, fewer false alerts, and more useful footage.

## The 4 rules of camera placement

1. **Cover entry points first** — Doors and windows are the most important areas
2. **Height matters** — Place cameras 7-8 feet high when possible for wider coverage
3. **Avoid backlighting** — Don't point the camera at windows or bright lights
4. **Test Wi-Fi first** — Check signal strength at your intended location before mounting

## Priority locations (in order)

### 1. Front door

The most important spot. The majority of break-ins and all deliveries happen at the front door.

- **Angle**: Point the camera at the door from across the room, or from a shelf/table near the door looking outward
- **Height**: Eye level or slightly above (5-7 feet)
- **Tip**: If placing inside looking out through a window, press the phone against the glass to reduce glare and reflections

### 2. Back door / side entrance

The second most common entry point.

- Place the camera where it can see the door and a few feet of surrounding area
- Indoor placement looking out through glass works well here too

### 3. Main hallway

If someone enters your home, they'll likely pass through a main hallway or corridor.

- Place the camera at one end of the hallway, facing down its length
- This gives you a clear view of anyone moving through the home

### 4. Living room / common area

Covers the main space where valuables are typically located.

- Place on a high shelf or bookcase corner
- Angle downward slightly for maximum room coverage

### 5. Garage

Especially useful if your garage is attached to the house or contains valuables.

- Mount near the ceiling pointed at the garage door
- Make sure Wi-Fi signal reaches (garages can have weak coverage)

### 6. Baby's room / pet area

Popular non-security use case.

- Place at a safe distance from the crib or pet area
- Make sure the power cable is out of reach of children and pets
- Use two-way audio for a baby monitor function

## Indoor placement guide

### Optimal angles

- **45-degree angle from the corner** captures the most room area
- Place the camera in a corner pointing diagonally across the room
- This eliminates blind spots on both adjacent walls

### Lighting considerations

- **Face the camera away from windows** to avoid backlighting
- Natural light from the side is ideal
- For night monitoring, consider a small night light in the room (since most iPhone apps lack true infrared night vision)
- Avoid pointing directly at lamps or light fixtures

### Mounting options

You don't need to drill holes. Here are the easiest mounting solutions:

- **Bookshelf**: Lean the phone against books on a high shelf
- **Phone stand**: A simple $5 phone stand on a shelf or table
- **Adhesive mount**: Stick-on phone mounts work on walls and cabinets
- **Tripod**: A small tabletop tripod gives adjustable angles
- **Phone clamp mount**: Clips to shelves, door frames, or railings
- **DIY**: Lean against any stable surface at the right angle

### Cable management

Since the phone will likely be plugged in:

- Run the charging cable along the wall or baseboard
- Use adhesive cable clips to keep it tidy
- Consider a longer cable (6-foot or 10-foot) for flexible placement
- Use a cable channel or cord cover for a clean look

## Outdoor placement considerations

iPhones are not weatherproof. If you want to monitor outdoor areas:

### Option A: Indoor camera looking out

- Place the phone against a window looking outward
- Press the phone flat against the glass to reduce reflections
- Turn off indoor lights near the window to minimize glare
- This is the safest approach (phone stays protected)

### Option B: Covered outdoor area

- Place the phone in a covered area (porch, carport, covered patio)
- Use a waterproof phone case for basic protection
- Make sure the camera lens area of the case is clear
- Ensure a power source is nearby

### Option C: Weatherproof enclosure (advanced)

- Purchase a weatherproof phone mount or enclosure
- These are available for $15-25 online
- Make sure it allows airflow to prevent overheating
- Run a charging cable through a window or door seal

**Note:** Outdoor placement exposes the phone to temperature extremes. iPhones perform best between 32-95 degrees F (0-35 degrees C). Extreme cold can drain the battery rapidly, and extreme heat can cause shutdowns.

## Wi-Fi signal optimization

Your camera is only as reliable as its Wi-Fi connection.

### Check signal strength

Before committing to a location:

1. Take the old iPhone to your intended camera spot
2. Go to **Settings > Wi-Fi** and check the signal indicator
3. Full bars = excellent. 2+ bars = good. 1 bar = risky.

### Improve weak signal

If the signal is weak at your desired location:

- **Move the camera closer to the router** (even a few feet helps)
- **Use a Wi-Fi extender** ($15-30, plugs into an outlet near the camera)
- **Switch to 2.4 GHz** instead of 5 GHz (longer range through walls)
- **Use a mesh Wi-Fi system** for whole-home coverage
- **Reduce interference** by moving away from microwaves, baby monitors, and other electronics

### Test reliability

After placing the camera:

1. Monitor the feed for 24 hours
2. Check for any disconnections or quality drops
3. If the feed drops frequently, move the camera closer to the router or add a Wi-Fi extender

## Multi-camera setup tips

If you have multiple old iPhones:

- Each phone needs its own camera app account (or use the same account depending on the app)
- Name each camera clearly: "Front Door," "Back Yard," "Baby Room"
- Stagger motion detection sensitivity — front door can be more sensitive than the living room
- Check that your Wi-Fi router can handle multiple streaming devices without slowing down

## Final placement checklist

- [ ] Camera covers the most important entry point
- [ ] Angle captures maximum area without blind spots
- [ ] Camera faces away from bright light sources
- [ ] Wi-Fi signal is strong at the chosen location
- [ ] Power cable reaches without being a tripping hazard
- [ ] Camera is mounted securely (won't fall)
- [ ] Camera view tested on viewer phone (correct angle and coverage)
- [ ] Motion detection tested (walk through the frame and verify alert)
