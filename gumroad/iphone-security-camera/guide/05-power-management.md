# Chapter 5: Power Management

An always-on camera needs reliable power. This chapter covers how to keep your iPhone running 24/7 while protecting battery health and managing heat.

## Understanding battery wear

When you use an iPhone as an always-on camera, the battery will degrade faster than normal use. This is expected and acceptable — you're repurposing a phone that was already retired. The goal is to minimize unnecessary wear while keeping the camera running reliably.

Key facts about lithium-ion batteries:
- They degrade fastest when kept at 100% or 0% charge
- Heat accelerates degradation
- Charge cycles (full 0-100%) cause more wear than partial cycles
- Modern iOS includes built-in protections

## Always-on plugged-in setup (recommended)

The simplest and most reliable approach: keep the phone plugged in at all times.

### Equipment you need

- **Charging cable**: Use an Apple-certified (MFi) Lightning or USB-C cable. Avoid cheap uncertified cables — they can overheat or deliver inconsistent power.
- **Power adapter**: A 5W or 10W adapter is fine. You don't need fast charging — slower charging generates less heat.
- **Surge protector**: Plug the adapter into a surge protector, not directly into the wall. This protects against power surges.

### Optimized charging

iOS includes Optimized Battery Charging, which learns your habits and delays charging past 80% until it predicts you'll need it. For an always-plugged-in camera:

1. Go to **Settings > Battery > Battery Health & Charging**
2. Keep **Optimized Battery Charging** turned **on**
3. iOS will manage the charge level intelligently

On newer iPhones (iPhone 15 and later with iOS 17+), you can also set a charge limit:
1. **Settings > Battery > Battery Health & Charging > Charge Limit**
2. Set to **80%** — this significantly extends battery lifespan

### Heat management

Heat is the biggest enemy of battery health when running an always-on camera.

**Placement rules:**
- Place the phone on a hard surface (wood, metal, tile) that conducts heat away
- **Never** place on fabric, carpet, or soft surfaces that trap heat
- **Never** place in direct sunlight
- **Never** place in enclosed spaces without airflow
- Keep away from heat sources (radiators, heating vents, electronics that generate heat)

**Active cooling (optional):**
- Remove any phone case — cases trap heat
- Use a small USB desk fan pointed at the phone ($5-10)
- A metal phone stand helps dissipate heat
- In hot climates, position near air conditioning or in a naturally cool area

**Monitor temperature:**
- If the phone displays a temperature warning, move it to a cooler location immediately
- Check the phone occasionally for excessive warmth
- Apps like Battery Health can track temperature over time

## Battery-only setup

If there's no power outlet near your desired camera location, you can run on battery — but with limitations.

### Maximize battery life

- Use **AtHome Camera** (lowest battery drain)
- Set video resolution to the lowest acceptable quality
- Enable **motion-triggered recording only** (no continuous recording)
- Turn screen brightness to absolute minimum
- Disable all features you don't need (two-way audio, cloud sync)
- Close all other apps

### Expected battery life

Rough estimates based on our testing:

| iPhone Model | Continuous Recording | Motion-Triggered Only |
|-------------|---------------------|----------------------|
| iPhone 6s/7 | 3-4 hours | 8-12 hours |
| iPhone 8/X | 4-5 hours | 10-15 hours |
| iPhone XR/11 | 5-7 hours | 14-20 hours |
| iPhone 12/13 | 6-8 hours | 16-24 hours |

These estimates assume a healthy battery. Degraded batteries will perform worse.

### Extending battery-only usage

- Use **Low Power Mode**: Settings > Battery > Low Power Mode (but note this may limit background activity for some camera apps)
- Use a **portable power bank** (10,000 mAh can add 2-3 full charges)
- Set up a **recording schedule** in the app to only monitor during specific hours

## Weekly maintenance routine

Set a recurring weekly reminder (Sunday evening works well) to:

1. **Restart the phone** — Hold the power button, slide to power off, wait 30 seconds, turn back on. This clears memory and resets processes.
2. **Check battery health** — Settings > Battery > Battery Health. Note the Maximum Capacity percentage.
3. **Check storage** — Settings > General > iPhone Storage. Delete old recordings if needed.
4. **Check for app updates** — Open the App Store and update the camera app if available.
5. **Check Wi-Fi connection** — Make sure the phone is still connected to your network.
6. **Clean the camera lens** — Wipe with a soft cloth to maintain video quality.

## When to replace the battery

Check battery health periodically: Settings > Battery > Battery Health > Maximum Capacity.

- **90-100%**: Excellent. No action needed.
- **80-89%**: Good. Battery is aging normally. Continue use.
- **70-79%**: Fair. You may notice shorter battery-only runtime. Consider replacing if not always plugged in.
- **Below 70%**: Poor. Battery replacement recommended if the phone isn't always plugged in. (If always plugged in, it still works fine.)

Battery replacement options:
- Apple Store or Apple Authorized Service Provider: $50-89 depending on model
- Third-party repair shops: $30-60
- DIY replacement kits: $15-30 (requires some technical skill)

For an always-plugged-in camera, a degraded battery is not a major issue since the phone runs on wall power.

## Power outage planning

If your power goes out, your camera will run on battery until it dies.

**Preparation:**
- Keep a portable power bank (charged) near the camera as a backup
- Consider a small UPS (uninterruptible power supply) if continuous monitoring is critical
- Know that Wi-Fi will also go out during a power outage (unless your router has battery backup)

**After power returns:**
- Check that the camera app restarted automatically
- If not, open the app manually on the camera phone
- Verify the feed is visible on your viewer phone
